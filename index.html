Paestum

Inhabited before the time of the Greeks, by the time the Romans settled there building many of their signature urban structures, it was a treasured relic of Greek history. Of note to the elite of the Grand Tour would have been the Temple of Athena, which was converted to a center of Christian worship, and The Temple of Hera, arguably the best-preserved Doric temple “alive” today. Both structures were erected by the Romans. Although Paestum’s physical legacy is not as rich as Rome’s, the significance of its three largest structures have stood the test of time.

Castel Sant'Angelo

One of the most significant structures in Rome, simply due to the fact that it was a functional structure during all of its life, is Castel Sant’Angelo. Originally built as Hadrian’s Mausoleum, by its namesake to use as he and his family’s final resting place, it was fortified for use by countless Popes against strife from both within and outside of Rome. Even at the time of the Grand Tour, Holy Fathers still barricaded themselves inside of its walls.

The Pantheon

Perhaps the most famous of the multireligious structures in Rome, and perhaps elsewhere, few structures match the iconoclasm of the Pantheon. Commissioned during the reign of Augustus and completed under Hadrian, it was eventually converted from pagan temple into a site of Christian worship. Of other interest to Grand Tourists would have been its postmortem inhabitants, notably Rafael. To put it very elegantly, it is truly amazing to look through the hole (oculus) in its ceiling.

The Forum

The centerpiece of at least Roman civic life, nothing comes to mind more for contemporary and elite Victorian tourists alike than the forum and its surroundings. So many celebrated figures have been celebrated here in their lifetimes and so many culture-shaping events have taken place here that it is hard not to feel the world-shaping energy that emanates from the place itself. It is historically the center of Rome, meaning that Rome’s earliest artifacts and structures have been found there.

Trajan's Column

One of my favorite structures in Rome, due in part to the ornateness of its carvings but also to different statues that have ridden its peak is Trajan’s Column. Built to commemorate Trajan’s victories in battle, it is located in Trajan’s forum, as opposed to “the” Forum. In the context of the Grand Tour, because of its height, it was something that at least did not disappoint the travelers of several centuries ago, whereas other structures were often quarried or buried away. It seems nondescript in comparison to the Victor Emmanuel monument, so much so that I almost passed it, but I was excited to find it.

Colosseum

Although it was not as restored as it is today, the Colosseum was undoubtedly a marvel, to some extent, of the Grand Tour. For all visitors to Rome, it is simply awe-inspiring, albeit in a horrifying way, to imagine men fighting each other and wild beasts to the death inside. This must have been particularly hard to imagine when it had more rural and overgrown environs, which was the case for century upon century. It was also quarried for hundreds of years for the lime in the marble. It took my breath away as I saw it stepping out of the Metro station simply because of how iconic it is.

Piazza del Popolo

The first site that many travelers saw when entering from the north, Piazza del Popolo lies just inside the northern edge of the Aurelian Walls. It is home to the second largest and oldest obelisk in Rome and is in close proximity to the late Emperor Nero. It struck me how alike to St. Peter’s square it is, though it was only made an oval in the 19th century.

Hadrian's Villa

Although I spent much of my time here napping, the number of well preserved structures here baffled me. I could only imagine retreating here among the countless baths, fountains, and shaded walkways. This was what it was built for by the emperor Hadrian, with very good access to both aqueduct and natural sources of water, and connected to Rome by several main roads. By the time that the Grand Tour became the norm, most of the mobile valuables had been looted, although the travelling aristocrats managed to find marbles here to take home.

Capitoline Museums

Home to the noteworthy She Wolf bronze and the Dying Gaul, the Capitoline Museums is considered to be one of the first museums in the world. This openness of display with regard to items of Roman history was crucial to the Grand Tour, as not everyone had access to the private collections of the Italian Aristocracy. It was originally conceived by Michelangelo but not opened to the public until the 18th century.

Villa D’Este

One of my favorite sites of the entire trip was the Villa D’Este, a magnificent, terraced Renaissance garden located in Tivoli. Although at the time of the Grand Tour, most of the gardens and fountains had fallen into disrepair. In the nineteenth century, restoration began that would lead the functionality and beauty of the villa and its gardens to restoration. Of particular charm, I though, was the water organ that plays daily.
